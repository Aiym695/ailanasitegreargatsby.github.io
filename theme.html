1. The American Dream: Idealism vs. Corruption

At the heart of The Great Gatsby lies a deep critique of the American Dream—the belief that anyone can achieve success, happiness, and upward mobility through hard work and determination. Jay Gatsby is its most iconic representation: born into poverty, he reinvents himself into a wealthy, mysterious socialite in hopes of winning back Daisy Buchanan, the woman he loves.

However, Fitzgerald shows that by the 1920s, the dream had become corrupted. Gatsby gains his wealth through illegal means (bootlegging), and his vision of success is entirely based on materialism and illusion.

"Gatsby believed in the green light, the orgastic future that year by year recedes before us."

2. Illusion vs. Reality

Fitzgerald emphasizes how characters in the novel live in a world built on fantasy, lies, and appearances. Gatsby fabricates a false identity... Gatsby loses Daisy, and his dream, built on illusion, crumbles.

3. Class and Social Stratification

  • Old Money – East Egg (Tom and Daisy Buchanan)
  • New Money – West Egg (Gatsby)
  • Working Class – Valley of Ashes (George and Myrtle Wilson)

Despite Gatsby’s immense wealth, he is never fully accepted by the old-money elite...

4. The Hollowness of the Upper Class

The novel shows that beneath the glamour of Gatsby’s parties lies moral emptiness and selfishness.

“They were careless people, Tom and Daisy — they smashed up things and creatures and then retreated back into their money...”

5. Love, Desire, and Obsession

Gatsby’s love for Daisy is based on obsession and idealism rather than true emotional connection...

6. Time and the Past

One of Gatsby’s defining traits is his belief that he can recreate the past.

“Can’t repeat the past?” he cried incredulously. “Why of course you can!”

7. Identity and Reinvention

Gatsby's transformation from James Gatz into Jay Gatsby shows the theme of self-invention—but built on deception.

8. Moral Decay and Materialism

The novel critiques a society more concerned with status and appearance than ethics, showing how that leads to isolation and destruction.
